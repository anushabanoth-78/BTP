Barbara Liskov

Institute Professor of Computer Science, MIT
Turing Award Winner 2008

Programming Languages Distributed Systems Software Engineering Data Abstraction

About Barbara Liskov

Barbara Liskov is a pioneering computer scientist best known for introducing the concepts of data abstraction and object-oriented programming, which transformed the foundations of modern software engineering.

She was the first woman in the United States to earn a Ph.D. in Computer Science (Stanford, 1968) and has been a major influence in programming language design through her work on the CLU language and distributed computing. Her Liskov Substitution Principle remains fundamental to object-oriented design.

Major Achievements

  • Recipient of the Turing Award (2008) ‚Äî the highest honor in computer science
  • Developed the Liskov Substitution Principle (LSP), a key idea in object-oriented design
  • First woman in the United States to earn a Ph.D. in Computer Science
  • Elected to the U.S. National Academy of Engineering and the American Academy of Arts and Sciences
  • IEEE John von Neumann Medal recipient for fundamental contributions to programming languages

Research Interests

  • Programming methodology and language design
  • Distributed and parallel systems
  • Programming languages and type systems
  • Software reliability and robustness
  • Byzantine fault tolerance

Key Contributions

  • CLU programming language introducing data abstraction
  • Argus distributed programming language
  • Byzantine fault tolerance algorithms
  • Fundamental work on abstract data types
  • Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance (PBFT) system

Biography

Robert S. Langer is an Institute Professor at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) ‚Äî the highest academic title awarded to only a few distinguished faculty members. He earned his Sc.D. in Chemical Engineering from MIT and has dedicated his career to developing new materials and methods for drug delivery and tissue engineering.

His groundbreaking work on polymer-based drug delivery systems has transformed the treatment of cancer, diabetes, and cardiovascular diseases. Langer's research also contributed significantly to mRNA vaccine development, including the COVID-19 vaccines produced by Moderna, which he co-founded.
